Atomic Structure
Protons · Neutrons · Electrons · Orbitals
⌄

Summary

An atom consists of a dense nucleus containing protons (positive) and neutrons (neutral), surrounded by shells of electrons (negative). The atomic number (Z) = number of protons. The mass number (A) = protons + neutrons. Isotopes share the same Z but differ in neutrons.

  • Electron shells fill in order: 2, 8, 8, 18…
  • Subshells: s (2e⁻), p (6e⁻), d (10e⁻)
  • Valence electrons determine chemical behaviour

Worked Example

Q: Write the electron configuration of Oxygen (Z=8) and determine its valence electrons.


Solution:
Total electrons = 8
Fill shells: 1st shell → 2e⁻ ; 2nd shell → 6e⁻

O: 2, 6 → or 1s² 2s² 2p⁴

Valence electrons = 6 (outermost shell). Oxygen needs 2 more to complete its octet, so it forms 2 bonds.

Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
Write the electron configuration of Sodium (Z = 11) and state how many valence electrons it has.
▷ Show Answer
Na: 2, 8, 1 — It has 1 valence electron. It readily loses this to form Na⁺.
Q2 Easy
How many neutrons does Chlorine-35 (Z = 17) have?
▷ Show Answer
Neutrons = A − Z = 35 − 17 = 18 neutrons.
Q3 Medium
Carbon-12 and Carbon-14 are isotopes. Explain what this means and state one use of Carbon-14.
▷ Show Answer
Both have Z = 6 (6 protons) but different mass numbers — C-12 has 6 neutrons; C-14 has 8. Carbon-14 is radioactive and is used in radiocarbon dating of organic materials.
Q4 Hard
An ion X²⁻ has 18 electrons. Identify element X, give its atomic number, and write its electron configuration before ionisation.
▷ Show Answer
X²⁻ has 18 electrons → neutral atom has 18 − 2 = 16 electrons → Sulfur (S), Z = 16. Electron config: 2, 8, 6 (or 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁴).

The Periodic Table
Groups · Periods · Trends · Classification
⌄

Summary

Elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic number. Periods (rows) show elements with the same number of electron shells. Groups (columns) share valence electron count and similar chemical properties.

  • Group 1: Alkali metals — very reactive, form +1 ions
  • Group 7: Halogens — reactive non-metals, form −1 ions
  • Group 0: Noble gases — full outer shells, mostly inert
  • Trends: atomic radius ↓ across period, ↑ down group
  • Ionisation energy ↑ across period, ↓ down group
Worked Example

Q: Explain why Sodium (Na) is more reactive than Lithium (Li) using periodic trends.


Answer: Both are in Group 1. Na is in Period 3, Li in Period 2. Na has more electron shells → its outer electron is further from the nucleus → weaker nuclear attraction → more easily lost. Therefore Na is more reactive (lower ionisation energy).

Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
Which group do the Noble gases belong to, and why are they unreactive?
▷ Show Answer
Group 0. They have a full outer electron shell (2 or 8 electrons), so they have no tendency to gain or lose electrons.
Q2 Medium
Arrange F, Cl, Br in order of increasing reactivity and explain the trend.
▷ Show Answer
Br < Cl < F (increasing reactivity). Going up Group 7, atoms become smaller → outer shell is closer to nucleus → stronger attraction for an incoming electron → more reactive.
Q3 Hard
Element X is in Period 3, Group 2. Write its electron configuration, predict the formula of its oxide, and explain whether it is a metal or non-metal.
▷ Show Answer
X = Magnesium (Mg). Config: 2, 8, 2. Mg forms Mg²⁺; oxygen forms O²⁻ → oxide formula: MgO. Mg is a metal (Group 2, left side of periodic table, loses electrons).

Chemical Bonding
Ionic · Covalent · Metallic · Intermolecular
⌄

Summary

Atoms bond to achieve stable full outer shells.

  • Ionic bonds: electron transfer between metal & non-metal → oppositely charged ions attract
  • Covalent bonds: electron sharing between non-metals → single, double, triple bonds
  • Metallic bonds: delocalised electrons in a lattice of positive ions → conducts electricity
  • Intermolecular forces: van der Waals, dipole-dipole, hydrogen bonds
Worked Example

Q: Explain the formation of sodium chloride (NaCl).


Na (Group 1) has 1 valence electron; Cl (Group 7) has 7. Na transfers its electron to Cl.

Na → Na⁺ + e⁻
Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻

The opposite charges attract strongly, forming an ionic lattice. NaCl has a high melting point (801 °C) due to this electrostatic attraction.

Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
State the type of bond in each: H₂O, MgO, Fe, HCl.
▷ Show Answer
H₂O → covalent; MgO → ionic; Fe → metallic; HCl → covalent (polar).
Q2 Medium
Draw a dot-and-cross diagram for methane (CH₄) and state the bond angle.
▷ Show Answer
Carbon (4 valence e⁻) forms 4 single covalent bonds with 4 hydrogen atoms. Each H shares 1 electron with C. The molecule is tetrahedral with bond angles of 109.5°.
Q3 Hard
Why does ice float on water? Explain using hydrogen bonding.
▷ Show Answer
In ice, every water molecule forms 4 hydrogen bonds in a rigid, open hexagonal lattice. This gives ice a lower density than liquid water (where H-bonds are constantly breaking/reforming, packing molecules closer). Hence ice floats.

Acids, Bases & pH
Neutralisation · pH scale · Indicators · Salts
⌄

Summary

  • Acid: proton (H⁺) donor; pH < 7; turns litmus red
  • Base: proton acceptor; pH > 7; turns litmus blue
  • Neutralisation: Acid + Base → Salt + Water
  • pH = −log₁₀[H⁺]; each unit = 10× concentration change
  • Strong acids fully dissociate (HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃)
  • Weak acids partially dissociate (CH₃COOH, H₂CO₃)
Worked Example

Q: Calculate the pH of a 0.01 mol/L HCl solution.


HCl is a strong acid → fully dissociates: [H⁺] = 0.01 mol/L = 10⁻² mol/L

pH = −log₁₀(10⁻²) = 2

The solution has pH = 2 (strongly acidic).

Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
Write the neutralisation equation for hydrochloric acid reacting with sodium hydroxide.
▷ Show Answer
HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O. The salt formed is sodium chloride.
Q2 Medium
Calculate the pH of a 0.001 mol/L H₂SO₄ solution. (Assume full dissociation; H₂SO₄ releases 2 H⁺.)
▷ Show Answer
[H⁺] = 2 × 0.001 = 0.002 mol/L → pH = −log(0.002) = 2.70.
Q3 Hard
25 cm³ of 0.1 mol/L NaOH is titrated with 0.2 mol/L HCl. What volume of HCl is needed for complete neutralisation?
▷ Show Answer
Moles NaOH = 0.025 × 0.1 = 0.0025 mol. Reaction 1:1 → moles HCl needed = 0.0025. Volume HCl = 0.0025 / 0.2 = 0.0125 L = 12.5 cm³.

Organic Chemistry
Hydrocarbons · Functional Groups · Reactions
⌄

Summary

  • Alkanes: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ — single bonds, saturated, combustion reactions
  • Alkenes: CₙH₂ₙ — double bond, unsaturated, addition reactions (bromine water test)
  • Alcohols: –OH group; ethanol (C₂H₅OH) — fermentation & combustion
  • Carboxylic acids: –COOH; react with alcohols → ester + water (esterification)
  • Cracking: breaking long alkanes into shorter, useful ones
Worked Example

Q: Write the equation for the complete combustion of propane (C₃H₈).

C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O

Balance: C: 3=3 ✓ H: 8=8 ✓ O: 10=10 ✓. Complete combustion produces only CO₂ and H₂O.

Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
Name the functional group in ethanol and state which homologous series it belongs to.
▷ Show Answer
Functional group: hydroxyl (–OH). Homologous series: alcohols.
Q2 Medium
Describe the bromine water test to distinguish between ethane and ethene.
▷ Show Answer
Add bromine water (orange) to each gas. Ethene decolourises bromine water (addition reaction across C=C). Ethane has no double bond → bromine water stays orange.
Q3 Hard
Write the equation for esterification of ethanol with ethanoic acid and name the ester formed.
▷ Show Answer
C₂H₅OH + CH₃COOH ⇌ CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O. Ester: ethyl ethanoate. Catalyst: concentrated H₂SO₄. Reaction is reversible.

Cell Biology
Prokaryotes · Eukaryotes · Organelles · Transport
⌄

Summary

  • Prokaryotic cells: no membrane-bound nucleus, no mitochondria (bacteria)
  • Eukaryotic cells: nucleus, mitochondria, ER, ribosomes (animals & plants)
  • Plant cells also have: cell wall (cellulose), chloroplasts, large central vacuole
  • Diffusion: passive; high → low concentration
  • Osmosis: water diffusion across semi-permeable membrane
  • Active transport: against gradient; needs energy (ATP)

Worked Example

Q: A red blood cell is placed in a 0.9% saline solution. Explain what happens.


0.9% saline is isotonic to blood plasma — the concentration inside and outside the cell is equal. There is no net movement of water by osmosis. The red blood cell maintains its normal biconcave shape.

Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
State three differences between plant and animal cells.
▷ Show Answer
Plant cells have: (1) cell wall (cellulose); (2) chloroplasts; (3) a large central vacuole. Animal cells have none of these.
Q2 Medium
Explain what happens to a plant cell placed in a very concentrated salt solution.
▷ Show Answer
The solution is hypertonic. Water leaves the cell by osmosis. The cell membrane pulls away from the cell wall — this is called plasmolysis. The cell becomes flaccid.
Q3 Hard
Compare active transport and facilitated diffusion, giving one example of each in human biology.
▷ Show Answer
Facilitated diffusion: passive (no ATP), along concentration gradient, via channel proteins. Example: glucose absorption into red blood cells. Active transport: requires ATP, against gradient, via carrier proteins. Example: sodium-potassium pump in nerve cells.

Genetics & Inheritance
DNA · Meiosis · Mendelian genetics · Mutations
⌄

Summary

  • DNA: double helix; bases A-T, C-G; genes = sections of DNA coding for proteins
  • Alleles: different versions of a gene; dominant (B) vs recessive (b)
  • Genotype (BB, Bb, bb) → Phenotype (expressed trait)
  • Homozygous (BB/bb) vs Heterozygous (Bb)
  • Monohybrid cross: use Punnett square to predict ratios
  • Mutations: random changes in DNA sequence
Worked Example — Punnett Square

Q: Brown eyes (B) are dominant over blue eyes (b). Two heterozygous parents cross. Predict the offspring ratio.


Parents: Bb × Bb

BB : Bb : bb = 1 : 2 : 1

Phenotype ratio: 3 brown : 1 blue. Probability of blue eyes = 25%.

Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
Define: allele, dominant, recessive, homozygous.
▷ Show Answer
Allele: alternative form of a gene. Dominant: expressed when one or two copies present. Recessive: only expressed when two copies present (homozygous). Homozygous: both alleles identical (AA or aa).
Q2 Medium
Cystic fibrosis (f) is recessive. Two carrier parents (Ff × Ff) have children. What is the probability of a child having cystic fibrosis?
▷ Show Answer
Punnett: FF, Ff, Ff, ff → 1 in 4 chance. Probability = 25%. Carriers (Ff) = 50%.
Q3 Hard
Explain how a mutation in the gene coding for haemoglobin leads to sickle cell anaemia.
▷ Show Answer
A single base substitution (point mutation) changes the codon for glutamic acid to valine. This produces abnormal haemoglobin (HbS). HbS molecules clump together under low oxygen, causing red blood cells to become sickle-shaped → they block capillaries and break down faster → anaemia and pain crises.

Photosynthesis
Light reactions · Calvin cycle · Limiting factors
⌄

Summary

Photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy (glucose).

6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
  • Light-dependent stage: in thylakoid membranes; splits water (photolysis); produces ATP & NADPH; releases O₂
  • Light-independent (Calvin cycle): in stroma; CO₂ fixed into G3P → glucose; uses ATP & NADPH
  • Limiting factors: light intensity, CO₂ concentration, temperature
Worked Example

Q: A plant is given extra CO₂ but the rate of photosynthesis doesn't increase. Suggest a reason.


Even though CO₂ is available, another factor is limiting — most likely light intensity or temperature. If there isn't enough light energy to produce ATP and NADPH, the Calvin cycle cannot run faster regardless of CO₂ levels.

Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
State the raw materials and products of photosynthesis.
▷ Show Answer
Raw materials: CO₂ (from air), H₂O (from soil), light energy. Products: glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆), oxygen (O₂).
Q2 Medium
Describe what happens to the rate of photosynthesis as light intensity increases from 0.
▷ Show Answer
Rate increases linearly with light intensity (light is limiting factor). Eventually it plateaus — another factor (CO₂ or temperature) now limits the rate regardless of further light increase.
Q3 Hard
Explain why a variegated (green and white) leaf has lower photosynthesis in white regions.
▷ Show Answer
White regions lack chlorophyll (and chloroplasts). Chlorophyll is the pigment that absorbs light energy. Without it, no light-dependent reactions can occur → no ATP/NADPH produced → no Calvin cycle → no glucose synthesised.

Human Body Systems
Circulatory · Digestive · Nervous · Respiratory
⌄

Summary

  • Circulatory: double circulation; heart → lungs (pulmonary); heart → body (systemic); arteries (away), veins (toward), capillaries (exchange)
  • Digestive: mouth → oesophagus → stomach → small intestine → large intestine; enzymes digest food; villi absorb nutrients
  • Nervous: CNS (brain + spinal cord) + PNS; neurones; reflex arc (stimulus → receptor → relay → motor → effector)
  • Respiratory: breathing (ventilation) + gas exchange in alveoli; O₂ in, CO₂ out
Worked Example

Q: Explain how the alveoli are adapted for efficient gas exchange.


  • Large surface area — millions of alveoli increase total area
  • Thin walls (one cell thick) — short diffusion distance
  • Rich capillary network — maintains concentration gradient
  • Moist lining — gases dissolve for diffusion
Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
State the role of valves in the heart and veins.
▷ Show Answer
Valves prevent backflow of blood. In the heart they separate chambers; in veins they stop blood falling back between heartbeats (veins have low pressure).
Q2 Medium
Describe the reflex arc when you touch a hot object.
▷ Show Answer
Heat stimulates receptor (skin) → impulse travels via sensory neurone → relay neurone in spinal cord → motor neurone → effector (arm muscle contracts, hand withdraws). This bypasses the brain for speed.
Q3 Hard
Compare the composition of blood in the pulmonary artery and pulmonary vein.
▷ Show Answer
Pulmonary artery: carries deoxygenated blood (high CO₂, low O₂) from right ventricle to lungs. Pulmonary vein: carries oxygenated blood (high O₂, low CO₂) from lungs to left atrium. Note: arteries carry blood away from heart — not always oxygenated.

Evolution & Natural Selection
Darwin · Adaptation · Speciation · Evidence
⌄

Summary

  • Evolution = change in allele frequency over generations
  • Natural selection: variation → competition → survival of fittest → reproduction → inherited traits
  • Adaptation: structural, physiological, behavioural
  • Speciation: geographic isolation → divergence → reproductive isolation → new species
  • Evidence: fossil record, comparative anatomy, DNA comparison, antibiotic resistance
Worked Example

Q: Explain how antibiotic resistance evolves in bacteria using natural selection.


  1. Random mutations give some bacteria resistance
  2. Antibiotic kills non-resistant bacteria (selection pressure)
  3. Resistant bacteria survive and reproduce
  4. Resistance allele passed to offspring — frequency increases
  5. Eventually, population is mostly resistant
Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
State four pieces of evidence for evolution.
▷ Show Answer
(1) Fossil record — shows change over time; (2) Comparative anatomy — homologous structures; (3) DNA sequences — related species share more DNA; (4) Direct observation — antibiotic/pesticide resistance.
Q2 Medium
Explain why the peppered moth population became predominantly dark during the industrial revolution.
▷ Show Answer
Soot darkened tree bark. Light moths became more visible to predators → eaten more → lower survival. Dark moths better camouflaged → survived and reproduced more → dark allele increased in frequency over generations.
Q3 Hard
Explain the process of allopatric speciation with an example.
▷ Show Answer
A population is geographically isolated (e.g. a mountain range or river separates two groups of squirrels). Each group faces different selection pressures → different mutations accumulate → populations diverge genetically. Eventually they become so different they can no longer interbreed = new species (reproductive isolation). Example: Galápagos finches — different islands, different beak shapes evolved.

Worked Example

Q: A ball is dropped from rest at height 45 m. Find its velocity when it hits the ground. (g = 10 m/s²)


u = 0, a = 10 m/s², s = 45 m, find v:

v² = u² + 2as = 0 + 2(10)(45) = 900
v = √900 = 30 m/s
Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
A car accelerates from 0 to 20 m/s in 5 s. Calculate the acceleration and net force if mass = 800 kg.
▷ Show Answer
a = Δv/t = 20/5 = 4 m/s². F = ma = 800 × 4 = 3200 N.
Q2 Medium
A 2 kg ball moving at 6 m/s collides with a stationary 4 kg ball. If they stick together, find the final velocity.
▷ Show Answer
Conservation of momentum: p_before = 2×6 = 12 kg·m/s. Total mass after = 6 kg. v = 12/6 = 2 m/s.
Q3 Hard
A projectile is fired horizontally at 20 m/s from a cliff 80 m high. Find (a) time to land and (b) horizontal range. (g = 10 m/s²)
▷ Show Answer
(a) Vertical: s = ½gt² → 80 = ½(10)t² → t² = 16 → t = 4 s. (b) Horizontal: x = 20 × 4 = 80 m.

Electricity & Circuits
Ohm's Law · Series/Parallel · Power · Charge
⌄

Summary

  • Ohm's Law: V = IR (Voltage = Current × Resistance)
  • Series: same current; voltages add; R_total = R₁ + R₂
  • Parallel: same voltage; currents add; 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂
  • Power: P = IV = I²R = V²/R
  • Charge: Q = It; Energy = QV = IVt
  • Kirchhoff: sum of voltages in loop = 0; currents at junction balance
Worked Example

Q: Two resistors, 4Ω and 6Ω, are connected in parallel across a 12V supply. Find the total resistance and total current.


1/R = 1/4 + 1/6 = 3/12 + 2/12 = 5/12
R = 12/5 = 2.4 Ω
I = V/R = 12/2.4 = 5 A
Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
A 9V battery powers a lamp of resistance 18Ω. Calculate the current and power.
▷ Show Answer
I = V/R = 9/18 = 0.5 A. P = IV = 0.5 × 9 = 4.5 W.
Q2 Medium
Three 6Ω resistors in series are connected to a 18V supply. Find (a) total resistance, (b) current, (c) voltage across each resistor.
▷ Show Answer
(a) R = 6+6+6 = 18 Ω. (b) I = 18/18 = 1 A. (c) V each = IR = 1×6 = 6 V (voltages add: 6+6+6=18 ✓).
Q3 Hard
A 60W light bulb runs for 3 hours on 230V mains. Calculate (a) current, (b) charge transferred, (c) energy used in kWh.
▷ Show Answer
(a) I = P/V = 60/230 = 0.26 A. (b) Q = It = 0.26 × (3×3600) = 2808 C. (c) E = 60W × 3h = 0.18 kWh.

Waves & Sound
Wave properties · EM spectrum · Reflection · Refraction
⌄

Summary

  • Wave equation: v = fλ (speed = frequency × wavelength)
  • Transverse waves: vibration ⊥ direction (light, water)
  • Longitudinal waves: vibration ∥ direction (sound)
  • EM spectrum (low→high freq): radio, microwave, IR, visible, UV, X-ray, gamma
  • Reflection: angle of incidence = angle of reflection
  • Refraction: speed change at boundary → bends toward normal when slowing
  • Diffraction: spreading of waves through gaps (gap ≈ wavelength → most diffraction)
Worked Example

Q: Sound travels at 340 m/s. A note has frequency 680 Hz. Calculate its wavelength.

λ = v/f = 340/680 = 0.5 m

The wavelength of this sound wave is 0.5 m. Since it is a longitudinal wave, particles vibrate parallel to the direction of travel.

Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
A radio wave has frequency 100 MHz. Calculate its wavelength. (c = 3×10⁸ m/s)
▷ Show Answer
λ = v/f = 3×10⁸ / 1×10⁸ = 3 m.
Q2 Medium
A ray of light hits a glass block at 40° to the normal. The refractive index of glass is 1.5. Calculate the angle of refraction.
▷ Show Answer
Snell's law: n₁sinθ₁ = n₂sinθ₂ → 1×sin40° = 1.5×sinθ₂ → sinθ₂ = 0.643/1.5 = 0.428 → θ₂ = 25.4°.
Q3 Hard
Explain why FM radio signals (λ ≈ 3m) diffract around buildings but light (λ ≈ 500nm) does not.
▷ Show Answer
Diffraction is most significant when the wavelength is comparable to the gap/obstacle size. FM waves (λ ≈ 3m) are similar in size to buildings → significant diffraction → signal bends around corners. Visible light (λ = 500nm) is vastly smaller than buildings → negligible diffraction → travels in straight lines past obstacles, causing shadows.

Radioactivity & Nuclear
α · β · γ radiation · Half-life · Fission & Fusion
⌄

Summary

  • Alpha (α): ⁴₂He nucleus; +2 charge; stopped by paper; most ionising
  • Beta (β): fast electron; −1 charge; stopped by 5mm aluminium
  • Gamma (γ): EM radiation; no charge; stopped by lead/thick concrete; least ionising
  • Half-life: time for half of nuclei to decay
  • Fission: large nucleus splits → energy released (nuclear reactors)
  • Fusion: small nuclei join → more energy released (stars, future reactors)
Worked Example

Q: A sample has 800 g of radioactive material with a half-life of 3 years. How much remains after 12 years?


Number of half-lives = 12/3 = 4

800 → 400 → 200 → 100 → 50 g

After 12 years, 50 g remains (each arrow = 1 half-life).

Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
State the charge, mass, and penetrating power of alpha, beta, and gamma radiation.
▷ Show Answer
Alpha: charge +2, mass 4u, stopped by paper. Beta: charge −1, mass ~0, stopped by aluminium. Gamma: charge 0, mass 0, reduced by lead/concrete.
Q2 Medium
Iodine-131 has a half-life of 8 days. A patient receives 320 MBq. What activity remains after 40 days?
▷ Show Answer
Half-lives in 40 days = 40/8 = 5. Activity = 320 × (½)⁵ = 320/32 = 10 MBq.
Q3 Hard
Compare nuclear fission and fusion in terms of fuel, products, energy released, and current technological use.
▷ Show Answer
Fission: fuel = uranium-235; products = 2 smaller nuclei + 2-3 neutrons + γ; energy from mass defect; used in nuclear power stations. Fusion: fuel = hydrogen isotopes (deuterium + tritium); product = helium-4 + neutron; releases ~4× more energy per kg; requires extreme temperature (≥10⁷K); currently experimental (ITER). Both release energy via E=mc².

Thermal Physics
Heat transfer · Specific heat · Gas laws · Thermodynamics
⌄

Summary

  • Heat transfer: conduction, convection, radiation
  • Specific heat capacity: Q = mcΔT (energy to raise temperature)
  • Latent heat: Q = mL (energy during change of state, no temp change)
  • Ideal gas law: PV = nRT; pressure ∝ temperature (constant V)
  • 1st Law: ΔU = Q − W (internal energy = heat in − work done by system)
  • 2nd Law: entropy of isolated system never decreases
Worked Example

Q: How much energy is needed to heat 2 kg of water from 20°C to 100°C? (c = 4200 J/kg°C)

Q = mcΔT = 2 × 4200 × 80 = 672 000 J

Energy required = 672 kJ. Note: this only heats the water to boiling point — extra latent heat is needed to actually vaporise it.

Practice Exercises
Q1 Easy
State the three methods of heat transfer and give one example of each.
▷ Show Answer
Conduction: metal spoon in hot soup. Convection: hot air rising from a radiator. Radiation: warmth from the Sun through space (electromagnetic waves, no medium needed).
Q2 Medium
A gas is compressed from 2 L to 0.5 L at constant temperature. If initial pressure was 1 atm, find the final pressure.
▷ Show Answer
Boyle's Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ → 1 × 2 = P₂ × 0.5 → P₂ = 4 atm.
Q3 Hard
1.5 kg of ice at 0°C is melted then heated to 60°C. Calculate total energy. (L_f = 334,000 J/kg; c_water = 4200 J/kg°C)
▷ Show Answer
Melting: Q₁ = mL = 1.5 × 334000 = 501 000 J. Heating: Q₂ = mcΔT = 1.5 × 4200 × 60 = 378 000 J. Total = 501 000 + 378 000 = 879 000 J = 879 kJ.
